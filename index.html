About Me

I am a third-year undergraduate student about to complete a Bachelor of Computer Science at Griffith University with a specific focus on distributed systems and networking. I am aspiring to become a software developer in the space and communication industries and completing my degree is only one of many steps.

I have always firmly believed that I would complete tertiary study one day, and although it took me a while to start, I know I have definitely made the right choice. My study at Griffith has brought me the opportunity to undertake a WIL placement with the space company, Gilmour Space Technologies. Never would I have thought that I would be working for a space company, but through Griffith, here on the Gold Coast I was able to. With Gilmour I was able to write software that will eventually go up on the ‘Joey’ satellite into space. I was involved in the design of specific modules that will allow network communication between the satellite and a ground station which will be essential for the sending and receiving of telemetry and command messages.

My goals are to continue the development of my skills in writing flight software for satellites by continuing to work with Gilmour if possible. I am looking at completing my Honours with courses and a thesis which are relevant to space communications and even continuing an to complete a PhD are top goals of mine. I think space is the ultimate frontier and would love to be a part of the pioneering push!

However, on that note I am still open to all and any opportunity to further my skills in these industries and welcome all prospective employers, leaders, entrepreneurs, and even other students to engage with me to see if we can collaborate in some way! Please have a read of my ePortfolio and if you have any questions don’t hesitate to reach out.

Critical analysis of WIL placement

Gilmour Space Technologies

My placement was an overall very enjoyable experience however at the beginning I did feel overwhelmed by what I had to learn. During the first few weeks I found myself confused over what to focus on and what I should prioritise as many things seemed equally as important.

I was juggling learning a new programming language, a new middleware system written in a different language, how to approach the programs I was meant to write, the paradigm of test-driven development, and the paradigm of deterministic real-time systems which is important for satellite software. I found it very challenging to synthesise all these different things and I felt quite lost over the days where I did not have face-to-face contact with the GSAT team, especially in the first few weeks.

However after several weeks of going to the placement office and attending the weekly meetings I gradually began to understand my task. I was able to ask questions from the senior software developers and another student doing their WIL placement with the team and this allowed me to get a grasp on how I could start approaching my placement deliverables. This reinforced to me the importance of making myself available at every opportunity to have face-to-face contact with other people and to ask as many questions as possible.

There were many opportunities where I did not need to be present at a meeting or I had the opportunity to work from home, but it proved very valuable to me that I did make the effort. This motivation is a skill I wish to solidify by reminding myself how it helped me get through the first few weeks of the placement.


I also thought that my code-writing skills would be quite good since I had spent the best part of the last three years writing lots of code. I had heard that the Swift programming language had a relatively easy learning curve, so I was happy to dive in. While I am confident in my ability to write code in Swift, writing code that follows good software engineering practices such as the SOLID principles is a skill I hope to develop further.

  • S - Single-responsibility Principle
  • O - Open-closed Principle
  • L - Liskov Substitution Principle
  • I - Interface Segregation Principle
  • D - Dependency Inversion Principle

After designing the class diagram shown above, I was able to use my knowledge of object-oriented programming to write the code for the socket structures that would be added to the SwiftCFS library that was being created by the GSAT software development team. The socket structures were Swift wrappers of the cFE middleware socket functions that were written in C. As this was my first-time writing wrapper code, I found it challenging as I wasn’t sure of what things I would need to account for. I had to take account of what a user of my software modules would want and what they would not care about.

The senior software developer who was supervising me gave me guidance throughout the process which gave me tremendous confidence and I felt very motivated to complete the task. My work went through many different code reviews, and I didn’t mind this. In fact, the code reviews were where I learnt the most. This process reminded me that while I have a decent understanding on how to write code, I need to develop my software engineering practices further. I am now much more invested in cementing my software engineering skills based on the SOLID principles, and so I am looking at staying on at Gilmour Space and continuing to work on my contribution to the SwiftCFS library over the summer period.

NASA core Flight System (cFS)

Read More

The cFS software framework is an industry-grade middleware developed by NASA that is used for satellite flight software. This framework is written in C and has many built in operating-system agnostic features including socket functionality. I had to learn how the cFS works to create a Swift interface for some of its functionality.

Test-driven software development

Read More

The GSAT team use test-driven development extensively which is very different to coding workflows that are learnt during the computer science program. This type of development requires a change of perspective and a more user-focused understanding of how to write code.
